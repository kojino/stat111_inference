(b) Derive the expectation of \(H_{a,b}\).

\[\begin{align*} E(H_{a,b})&=\frac{1}{n(b-a)} \sum_{i=1}^n E(\textbf{1}_{y_i \in (a,b]}) \quad (\because \text{linearity}) \\ &= \frac{1}{n(b-a)} \sum_{i=1}^n P(y_i \in (a,b]) \quad (\because \text{fundamental bridge, from Stat110}) \\ &= \frac{1}{n(b-a)} \sum_{i=1}^n F(b) - F(a) \quad (\because \text{definition of CDF}) \\ &= \frac{F(b) - F(a)}{b-a} \end{align*}\]

(c) Derive the variance of \(H_{a,b}\).

\[\begin{align*} Var(H_{a,b})&=\frac{1}{(n(b-a))^2} Var(\sum_{i=1}^n \textbf{1}_{y_{i \in (a,b]}}) \quad (\because \text{constant comes out squared}) \\ &=\frac{1}{(n(b-a))^2} \sum_{i=1}^n Var(\textbf{1}_{y_{i \in (a,b]}}) \quad (\because \text{$Y_i$ is iid}) \\ &=\frac{1}{(n(b-a))^2} \sum_{i=1}^n E(\textbf{1}_{y_{i \in (a,b]}}^2)-E(\textbf{1}_{y_{i \in (a,b]}})^2 \quad (\because \text{definition of variance}) \\ &= \frac{1}{(n(b-a))^2} \sum_{i=1}^n E(\textbf{1}_{y_{i \in (a,b]}})-E(\textbf{1}_{y_{i \in (a,b]}})^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{(n(b-a))^2} \sum_{i=1}^n (F(b) - F(a)) - (F(b) - F(a))^2 \quad (\because \text{(b)}) \\ &= \frac{(F(b)-F(a))-(F(b)-F(a))^2}{n(b-a)^2} \end{align*}\]

4. Census Income Data

The purpose of the question is to identify if there is an additive (or decremental) effect on income caused by divorce. This question can be useful, for example, when making advice to couples. It can also be useful for academic purposes, say for sociologists or economists who would like to understand the impact of divorce. The key point here is that we are not measuring the correlation between income and the rate of divorce. For example, if there exists a confounding factor, than correlation would not explain the impact of divorce on income. Hence, we need to ask a causal question.

The data might help in addressing this causal question only if we know how to use it. First, the data is likely observational, not randomized. Hence, a naive comparison of the average income in those who got divorced or not will not provide a valid causal effect. Applying methods like propensity score matching can overcome this issue.

5. Canonical Exponential Family

(a) Show that normal is in canonical exponential family.

\[\begin{align*} f(y|\eta) &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi c^2}}exp(-\frac{(y-\eta)^2}{2c^2}) &\\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi c^2}}exp(-\frac{1}{2c^2} (y^2 - 2y\eta + \eta^2)) &\\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi c^2}}exp(-\frac{1}{2c^2}y^2)exp(-\frac{y\eta}{c^2}-\frac{\eta^2}{2c^2}) \end{align*}\] Hence, \[\begin{align*} h(y) &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi c^2}}exp(-\frac{1}{2c^2}y^2) &\\ T(y) &= \frac{y}{c^2} &\\ A(\eta) &= -\frac{\eta^2}{2c^2} \end{align*}\]

(b) Show that binomial is in canonical exponential family.

\[\begin{align*} f(y|\eta) &= {n \choose y} p^y (1-p)^(1-y) &\\ &={n \choose y}exp(ylogp + (n-y)log(1-p)) &\\ &={n \choose y}exp(ylog(\frac{p}{1-p} + nlog(1-p)) \end{align*}\] Let \(\eta = log(\frac{p}{1-p})\). Then, \[\begin{align*} & \eta = log(\frac{p}{1-p}) &\\ \Leftrightarrow & e^{\eta} = \frac{p}{1-p} \\ \Leftrightarrow & \frac{1}{e^{\eta}} = \frac{1}{p} - 1 \\ \Leftrightarrow & p = \frac{e^{\eta}}{e^{\eta}+1} \\ \end{align*}\] Hence, \[\begin{align*} h(y) &= {n \choose y} &\\ T(y) &= y &\\ A(\eta) &= nlog(1-p) = -nlog(e^{\eta}+1) \end{align*}\]

(c)

\[\begin{align*} E(e^{\delta T(Y)}|\eta) &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{\delta T(y)} f(y|\eta) dy &\\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{\delta T(y)} h(y) e^{\eta T(y)-A(\eta)} dy &\\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{(\delta+\eta)T(y) -A(\eta)} h(y) dy &\\ &= (e^{A(\eta+\delta)-A(\eta)}) \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{(\delta+\eta)T(y)-A(\delta+\eta)} h(y) dy &\\ &= (e^{A(\eta+\delta)-A(\eta)}) \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(y|\delta+\eta) dy &\\ &= e^{A(\eta+\delta)-A(\eta)} \end{align*}\]

Hence, \[E(e^{\delta T(Y)}|\eta) = e^{A(\eta+\delta)-A(\eta)}\]

From the definition of MGF, \[ E(e^{\delta T(Y)}|\eta) = M_{T(Y)|\eta}(\delta) \] Since mean is the first moment, \[E(T(Y)|\eta)=M_{T(Y)|\eta}^{(1)}(0)\] Let’s evaluate \(M_{T(Y)|\eta}^{(1)}(\delta)\).

\[\begin{align*} M_{T(Y)|\eta}^{(1)}(\delta) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \delta} e^{A(\eta+\delta)-A(\eta)} \\ &= (\frac{\partial}{\partial \delta} A(\eta+\delta)) e^{A(\eta+\delta)-A(\eta)} \\ &= (\frac{\partial}{\partial \eta} A(\eta+\delta)) e^{A(\eta+\delta)-A(\eta)} \quad (\because \text{symmetry of $\eta$ and $\delta$}) \end{align*}\]

Hence, \[E(T(Y)|\eta)=M_{T(Y)|\eta}^{(1)}(0) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \eta} A(\eta)\]
